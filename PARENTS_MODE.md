### Your Reality:

## What Your Child Needs to Hear

### 1. **Unconditional Love**
"I love you. That will never change."

- Not: "I love you, but..."
- Not: "I love you when you..."
- Just: "I love you. Period."

### 2. **You Believe in Them**
"I believe in you and your future."

- Remind them this is not their whole story
- Talk about their potential
- Express confidence in their ability to change

### 3. **They're Not Alone**
"We will get through this together."

- Use "we" language
- Show you're in it with them
- They're not abandoned

### 4. **Pride in Current Choices**
"I'm proud you finished that class."

- Celebrate small wins
- Focus on now, not past
- Reinforce positive behavior

### 5. **They're More Than This**
"You are not your mistakes."

- Separate person from behavior
- Affirm their inherent worth
- Build identity beyond this experience

## What NOT to Say

### ‚ùå Avoid These:

1. **"How could you do this to me?"**
   - Makes it about you, not them
   - Increases shame
   - Better: "I know you're going through something hard"

2. **"You've ruined your life"**
   - Removes hope
   - Creates hopelessness
   - Better: "This is hard, but it's not your whole story"

3. **"Everyone is so ashamed of you"**
   - Piles on shame
   - Isolates them
   - Better: "We miss you and want you home"

4. **"Your brother/sister would never..."**
   - Damaging comparisons
   - Creates resentment
   - Better: Focus only on this child

5. **"I give up on you"**
   - Abandonment
   - Removes safety net
   - Better: "I'm frustrated, but I'm not leaving you"

6. **"This is your fault"**
   - They know
   - Adds guilt to shame
   - Better: "Let's focus on moving forward"

## Special Situations

### If Your Child Hurt You or a Family Member:

This is incredibly painful. You're allowed to have complex feelings.

**You can:**
- Set boundaries while maintaining connection
- Express hurt honestly while offering forgiveness
- Take time to process before writing
- Work with a therapist on your feelings
- Still love them while being hurt

**Try:**
"What happened hurt me deeply. I'm working through those feelings. 
But I want you to know - I still love you. I need time, and we'll 
need to rebuild trust. But I haven't given up on you or us."

---

### If Your Child Won't Respond:

Keep writing anyway.

- They're reading it (even if not responding)
- They might not know what to say
- They might feel too ashamed
- Your words still matter
- Don't give up

**Try:**
"I know you haven't written back. That's okay. I'll keep writing 
because I want you to know I'm thinking of you. No pressure to 
respond - just know I'm here."

---

### If You're Angry:

It's okay to be angry. But wait to write until you can be constructive.

- Write a draft first (don't send it)
- Wait 24 hours
- Rewrite with love leading
- It's okay to express disappointment - just balance with hope

**Try:**
"I'm struggling with a lot of feelings right now. I'm disappointed 
about what happened. But more than that, I'm worried about you and 
your future. Let's talk about how we move forward."

## Supporting Different Ages

### Younger Teens (13-15):
- Simpler language
- More reassurance
- Focus on daily life
- Remind them they're still a kid
- Don't expect adult reasoning

### Older Teens (16-18):
- More complex conversations
- Future planning (job, school)
- Young adult respect
- Acknowledge their growing maturity
- Discuss consequences more deeply

## Reentry Planning Through Letters

Start talking about the future in your messages:

### Topics to Discuss:
- **School:** Will they return? GED? Alternative programs?
- **Work:** Job interests? Skills to learn? Work experience?
- **Home:** House rules? Expectations? Support systems?
- **Treatment:** Therapy? Programs? Continued support?
- **Social:** Healthy friendships? Avoiding negative influences?

**Start these conversations early.** Don't wait until release.
